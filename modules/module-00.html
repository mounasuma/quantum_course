Module 00 · Foundation

Why Quantum Computing?

Before writing a single line of quantum code, you need to understand what problem quantum computing actually solves and why classical computers hit a wall.

⏱ ~45 minutes 📖 Reading only no code 🎯 Beginner

01 Start with a problem classical computers cannot solve

Let's say you need to find the shortest route connecting 20 cities visiting each city exactly once. Simple enough, right?

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the fastest classical computers on Earth cannot reliably solve this problem if the number of cities gets large enough. Not because the computers are slow. Because the number of possible routes grows so explosively that even checking one route per nanosecond would take longer than the age of the universe.

With 20 cities, there are approximately 1.2 quadrillion possible routes. With 50 cities? The number of routes exceeds the number of atoms in the observable universe.

💡 Key insight

This is not a hardware problem. You cannot solve it by buying a faster CPU or more RAM. The problem grows exponentially and no classical machine, however fast, can outrun exponential growth.

This class of problem where the difficulty explodes exponentially with size is exactly where quantum computing is designed to help. Not for every problem. Not even for most problems. But for specific problems with this exponential structure, quantum computers offer a fundamentally different approach.

02 What makes a classical computer "classical"?

Before understanding what quantum computing is, you need a clear picture of what classical computing is. And the answer is simpler than you think.

Every classical computer from your phone to the world's fastest supercomputer processes information using bits. A bit is a switch. It is either off (0) or on (1). That's it. The entire complexity of modern computing every app, every website, every AI model is built on billions of these switches flipping on and off, millions of times per second.

🧠 Analogy

Imagine a light switch. It can be off or on nothing in between. A classical computer is a city of billions of light switches, all flipping incredibly fast according to carefully designed rules. The rules are your software. The switches are your hardware.

The key property of a classical bit: it always has a definite value. It is 0. Or it is 1. There is no in-between. This seems obvious even trivial but it is the fundamental limitation we will later contrast with quantum computing.

Here is the constraint this creates: to find the best answer among many possible answers, a classical computer must check answers one at a time (or a few at a time with parallelism). For small problems, this is fine. For exponentially large problems, it becomes impossible.

03 The classical wall a concrete example

Let's make this concrete. Suppose you have a combination lock with 4 digits, each 0–9. That's 10,000 possible combinations. A classical computer can try all 10,000 in microseconds. No problem.

Now suppose the lock has 300 digits. The number of combinations is 10³⁰⁰ a number with 300 zeros after it. If every atom in the universe was a computer checking one billion combinations per second since the Big Bang, you would not have tried a fraction of the possible combinations.

This is the classical wall. It is not a wall we can break through by building faster chips. It is a mathematical wall built into the structure of how classical information works.

📌 The core problem: sequential search
Classical computers check possibilities sequentially. Even with billions of parallel processors, the exponential growth of problem space always eventually wins. Quantum computing offers a different strategy: explore many possibilities simultaneously by exploiting the physics of quantum mechanics.

04 What is "quantum" the physical basis

The word "quantum" comes from physics. It refers to the behaviour of matter and energy at the scale of individual atoms and particles a scale where physics works very differently from our everyday experience.

At the quantum scale, particles do not behave like billiard balls. They behave like waves. And waves can do something billiard balls cannot: they can be in multiple states at the same time.

This is not science fiction or metaphor. It is an experimentally verified fact about the physical world. An electron, for example, can genuinely be spinning in two directions simultaneously not "we don't know which direction", but actually both, until we observe it.

🧠 Analogy the wave

Drop a stone in a pond. The ripple spreads outward in all directions simultaneously it does not pick one direction and go there. An electron in superposition is like that ripple: it genuinely exists across multiple states at once. Only when you measure it like putting your hand in the pond does it "choose" a definite position.

Quantum computing is the engineering project of harnessing this quantum behaviour to process information. Instead of bits that are definitely 0 or 1, we use qubits quantum bits that can be in a superposition of 0 and 1 simultaneously.

This is not just a philosophical curiosity. It has a practical consequence: a system of n qubits can represent 2ⁿ states simultaneously. Ten qubits: 1,024 states. Fifty qubits: over one quadrillion states. Three hundred qubits: more states than there are atoms in the observable universe all held in superposition at once.

05 The quantum strategy a different way of computing

Here is the key shift in thinking: a classical computer solves problems by searching through possibilities one at a time. A quantum computer, in the right circumstances, can set up a superposition across all possibilities simultaneously and then use quantum interference to make the right answer more likely to appear when measured.

1
Superposition explore everything at once
Put qubits into a superposition of all possible answers. Now the system is "trying" every answer simultaneously.
2
Interference amplify the right answer
Apply quantum operations that increase the probability of correct answers and decrease the probability of wrong ones. Like noise-cancelling headphones, but for incorrect solutions.
3
Measurement read out the result
Measure the system. The superposition collapses to a single answer and because of step 2, it is probably the right one. Run many times to build confidence.
⚠ Important this is not magic

Quantum computers do not simply try all answers simultaneously and magically pick the best one. The interference step (step 2) is the hard part designing quantum circuits that actually amplify correct answers is a research problem. This is why quantum algorithms are difficult to design, and why quantum computers do not outperform classical computers on most problems.

06 What quantum computing is NOT

Before going further, let's clear up the misconceptions because the hype around quantum computing has created a lot of them.

🚫 Common misconceptions
✗  "Quantum computers are just faster classical computers"
✓  They are fundamentally different in how they compute faster on specific problems, completely useless on others
🚫 Common misconceptions
✗  "Quantum computers will replace classical computers"
✓  They will coexist as specialised coprocessors like GPUs. Classical computers will still run your apps, websites, and most software
🚫 Common misconceptions
✗  "Quantum supremacy means quantum computers are better than classical"
✓  "Supremacy" meant a quantum computer did one specific artificially constructed task faster not that it was generally better at anything useful
🚫 Common misconceptions
✗  "Quantum computers can crack all encryption right now"
✓  Shor's algorithm theoretically breaks RSA encryption but requires millions of error-corrected qubits. Today's machines have hundreds of noisy qubits. This threat is decades away at minimum

07 The honest picture where quantum helps and where it doesn't

Quantum computing has demonstrated or strongly theoretical advantages in four areas. It is important to be precise about this not "quantum will help with everything" but specific, honest claims.

Problem type Classical Quantum advantage Status
Unstructured search O(N) O(√N) Grover's Proven, limited by noise
Integer factoring Exponential Polynomial Shor's Proven theory, hardware decades away
Quantum simulation Exponential Polynomial VQE Small molecules today
Combinatorial optimisation Heuristics Approximate QAOA Classical still wins today
Machine learning Deep learning dominates Unclear research active No demonstrated advantage
Most software tasks Excellent None Classical wins, full stop
✓ The honest summary

We are in the NISQ era Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum. Machines with 100–1000 qubits exist today but they are noisy, error-prone, and limited in circuit depth. Real quantum advantage on practical problems remains years away for most applications. Learning quantum computing now means building expertise before the hardware catches up not because it will help you solve problems today.

08 The two paradigms you will encounter

Quantum computing is not one thing there are two fundamentally different hardware approaches, and this course covers both.

⚛️ Gate-model quantum computing
Used by IBM, Google, IonQ, Rigetti. Applies sequences of quantum gates (like logic gates in classical computing) to qubits. Programmable for any quantum algorithm. Tools: Qiskit, PennyLane, Cirq. This is the main paradigm in this course.
🌊 Quantum annealing
Used by D-Wave. Physically evolves a quantum system toward minimum energy which corresponds to the optimal solution of a problem. Not universal, but useful for specific optimisation problems. Tools: D-Wave Ocean SDK. Covered in Module 07.

You do not need to understand either of these deeply right now. Just know that they exist, they are different, and this course will teach you when to use which.
