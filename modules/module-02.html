Quantum Gates & Circuits

Classical logic gates mapped to quantum gates. You will build circuits, understand why circuit depth is the primary NISQ constraint, and see exactly how noise accumulates as circuits grow deeper.

01 From classical gates to quantum gates

In Module 01 you learned that a qubit can be in superposition unlike a classical bit, it has a direction rather than just a value. Now we need to do something with qubits. That is what gates are for.

In classical computing, logic gates manipulate bits. You already know AND, OR, and NOT from school or programming. A classical gate takes one or more bits as input and produces a bit as output. The NOT gate, for example, flips 0 to 1 and 1 to 0.

Quantum gates work the same way conceptually they are operations applied to qubits. But they have two critical differences from classical gates that change everything.

Difference 1: Quantum gates are reversible
A classical AND gate takes two bits and produces one bit information is permanently lost. Quantum gates are unitary: they preserve all information. Every quantum gate can be undone by applying its inverse. This is not a design choice it is a requirement of quantum mechanics.
Difference 2: Quantum gates rotate the Bloch sphere
Remember the Bloch sphere from Module 01? A quantum gate moves the state vector from one point on the sphere to another. It is a rotation. The Hadamard gate, for example, rotates |0⟩ from the north pole to the equator. This geometric picture is the most powerful way to understand what gates do.
🧠 Analogy

Classical gates are like light switches flip them and the state changes permanently, you cannot go back. Quantum gates are like rotating a compass needle apply the rotation, the needle moves, and you can always rotate it back to where it started. No information is ever destroyed.

02 Single-qubit gates the essential five

There are many quantum gates, but five appear in almost every circuit you will ever write. Learn these five and you can understand 90% of the quantum code you encounter.

Gate Name What it does Classical analogy
X Pauli-X / bit-flip Flips |0⟩ to |1⟩ and |1⟩ to |0⟩. Rotates 180° around X axis on Bloch sphere. Classical NOT gate but reversible
H Hadamard Takes |0⟩ to equal superposition |+⟩. Takes |1⟩ to |−⟩. The superposition engine. No classical equivalent uniquely quantum
Z Pauli-Z / phase-flip Leaves |0⟩ unchanged. Flips the phase of |1⟩. Invisible until combined with other gates. No classical equivalent
Rz(θ) Z-rotation Rotates the state vector by angle θ around the Z axis. Parameterised θ is a number you choose. No classical equivalent
S S gate / phase gate Rz(π/2) rotates 90° around Z axis. Used in QFT and many algorithms. No classical equivalent

The X gate is the one that feels most familiar it is the quantum NOT. But notice how different the H gate is from anything in classical computing. The Hadamard gate has no classical equivalent whatsoever. It genuinely creates superposition something that is impossible in classical logic. This is the gate you will see most often.

💡 Key insight

Applying H twice to the same qubit returns it to its original state H·H = Identity. This is a consequence of reversibility: every quantum gate has an inverse, and for H that inverse is H itself. Try this in the hands-on section.

03 Two-qubit gates entanglement starts here

Single-qubit gates only manipulate one qubit at a time. Two-qubit gates connect two qubits together and this is where the power of quantum computing begins to emerge.

The CNOT gate the most important two-qubit gate
CNOT stands for "Controlled-NOT." It takes two qubits: a control qubit and a target qubit. The rule is simple: if the control qubit is |1⟩, apply X (flip) to the target. If the control qubit is |0⟩, do nothing to the target. In a circuit diagram, CNOT is drawn as a dot on the control qubit connected by a vertical line to a circle-plus ⊕ on the target qubit.
H gate creates superposition on q₀, CNOT entangles q₁ to q₀. Result: a Bell state always correlated.

The H + CNOT combination you see above is the most important two-gate sequence in quantum computing. It creates a Bell state the simplest example of quantum entanglement. When you measure both qubits, you always get either 00 or 11, never 01 or 10. The qubits are correlated no matter how far apart they are. This is the foundation of Module 03.

04 Circuit depth the NISQ bottleneck

A quantum circuit is a sequence of gates applied to qubits. The depth of a circuit is the number of gate layers that cannot be parallelised the "length" of the longest path through the circuit.

Circuit depth matters because of noise. Every gate operation introduces a small error. On IBM's best hardware today, a single-qubit gate has about 0.3% error and a two-qubit gate has about 0.6% error. This sounds tiny but errors accumulate with every gate.

How errors accumulate the maths
If each gate has 99.5% fidelity (0.5% error), then:
• 10 gates: 0.995¹⁰ ≈ 95% total fidelity
• 50 gates: 0.995⁵⁰ ≈ 78% total fidelity
• 100 gates: 0.995¹⁰⁰ ≈ 61% total fidelity
• 200 gates: 0.995²⁰⁰ ≈ 37% total fidelity

At depth ~200, your results are essentially random noise. This is why NISQ algorithms must be shallow.

⚠ The NISQ reality

Most quantum algorithms you read about in papers require thousands of gate layers far beyond what NISQ hardware can execute reliably. Practical NISQ circuits today are limited to roughly 50–100 gate layers before noise dominates. This is the central engineering challenge of the era.

05 Transpilation your logical circuit vs real hardware

When you write a quantum circuit in Qiskit, you write it in terms of "logical" gates H, CNOT, X, and so on. But real quantum hardware does not support all these gates natively. Each chip has a small set of native gates it can physically execute.

IBM's quantum computers use a native gate set that includes CNOT (or its equivalent CX), and rotation gates like Rx, Ry, Rz. Any other gate you write must be decomposed into these native operations before running on hardware. This decomposition is called transpilation.

⚠ What transpilation does to your circuit
✗  "The circuit I write is the circuit that runs on hardware"
✓  Transpilation typically increases depth by 2–3× and gate count by 2–4×. A circuit with depth 20 may become depth 60 after transpilation.

This is why you always run transpile(qc, backend) before executing on real hardware it adapts your logical circuit to the physical constraints of the specific chip. In the hands-on section you will measure exactly how much transpilation inflates your circuit.

✓ The honest picture

Quantum gates are slower, noisier, and harder to build than classical gates. The only reason to use them is to access quantum effects superposition, entanglement, interference that give quantum algorithms their advantage on specific problems. For everything else, classical is better.

07 When does circuit depth actually matter?

Not all quantum algorithms need deep circuits. The algorithms that are most promising for near-term hardware are specifically designed to be shallow. Here is the honest picture:

Shallow circuits realistic today
QAOA (depth p=1–4), VQE with hardware-efficient ansatz (depth 10–30), simple Grover's search (small N), and quantum kernels for ML all can run on NISQ hardware at some scale.
Deep circuits need fault-tolerant hardware
Shor's algorithm for RSA-2048 (millions of gates), full Grover's search for large N, full quantum simulation of complex molecules all require error-corrected qubits that do not exist yet. These are decades away.

Define NOT, AND, and OR as one-line Python functions. Then print a truth table for each and show concretely that AND cannot be reversed three different input pairs all produce 0 as output, so there is no way to recover the original inputs from the result alone.

nb00_classical_gates.pypython nb00_classical_gates.py
# Classical gates simple boolean operations def NOT(a): return 1 - a def AND(a, b): return a & b def OR(a, b): return a | b # Print truth tables print("NOT gate:") for a in [0, 1]: print(f" NOT({a}) = {NOT(a)}") print("\nAND gate:") for a in [0, 1]: for b in [0, 1]: print(f" AND({a},{b}) = {AND(a,b)}") # Prove AND is irreversible: two inputs → one output print("\nAND(0,0) = AND(0,1) = AND(1,0) = 0") print("Given output=0, cannot recover which input produced it.") print("This is IRREVERSIBILITY information is permanently lost.")
Key observation
AND(0,0) = 0Output is 0. But so is AND(0,1) and AND(1,0). Given the output alone, you cannot recover the inputs. Information is destroyed.
NOT(0) = 1NOT is actually reversible given the output 1, you know the input was 0. NOT is the one classical gate that behaves like a quantum gate.
💡 Why this matters

The fact that AND destroys information means it cannot be implemented as a quantum gate. Quantum gates must be reversible they can only be operations that preserve all information. This is why quantum computing requires entirely different building blocks from classical computing.

H gate applied twice proving reversibility

Apply H once to create superposition, then apply H twice in a second circuit and confirm you get |0⟩ with 100% certainty. H is its own inverse two Hadamards cancel each other out, just like two X gates did.

nb01b_hh_identity.pypython nb01b_hh_identity.py
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator sim = AerSimulator() # H once: creates superposition → ~50/50 qc1 = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) qc1.h(0) qc1.measure(0, 0) c1 = sim.run(transpile(qc1, sim), shots=1000).result().get_counts() print("H|0⟩:", c1) # ~{'0': 500, '1': 500} # H twice: should return to |0⟩ proving H·H = Identity qc2 = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) qc2.h(0) qc2.h(0) # second H undoes the first qc2.measure(0, 0) c2 = sim.run(transpile(qc2, sim), shots=1000).result().get_counts() print("H·H|0⟩:", c2) # {'0': 1000} always 0 print("\nConclusion: H·H = Identity. All quantum gates are reversible.")

The H·H = Identity result is not obvious from the analogy two coin spins do not undo each other in the physical world. But in quantum mechanics, because gates are rotations on the Bloch sphere, and H is a 180° rotation about a specific axis, applying it twice returns to the start. This is the mathematical elegance of quantum gates.

Import Qiskit and create a circuit with 3 qubits and 3 classical bits. The classical bits are where measurement results get stored each qubit maps to one classical bit.

nb02_circuit.pystep 1 setup
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator import matplotlib.pyplot as plt qc = QuantumCircuit(3, 3)

Add gates to each qubit: H puts qubit 0 in superposition, X flips qubit 1 to |1⟩, CNOT makes qubit 0 control qubit 2, then H goes on qubit 1 again. Finally measure all three qubits. Then print the circuit as a text diagram and check the depth.

nb02_circuit.pystep 2 add gates
qc.h(0) # Superposition on qubit 0 qc.x(1) # Flip qubit 1 to |1⟩ qc.cx(0, 2) # CNOT: q0 controls q2 qc.h(1) # Superposition on qubit 1 qc.measure([0,1,2], [0,1,2]) print(qc.draw(output='text')) print(f"Depth: {qc.depth()} Gates: {qc.size()}")

Run the circuit 1000 times on the simulator and plot how often each of the 8 possible 3-bit output states appears. Because qubit 0 is in superposition and its state is linked to qubit 2 via CNOT, you will see two groups of states sharing the probability roughly equally.

nb02_circuit.pystep 3 run and plot
sim = AerSimulator() counts = sim.run(transpile(qc, sim), shots=1000).result().get_counts() print("Measurement counts:", counts) fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 4)) ax.bar(counts.keys(), [v/1000 for v in counts.values()], color='#1A4FDB', alpha=0.75, width=0.5) ax.set_ylabel('Probability') ax.set_title('3-qubit circuit measurement distribution') plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
What each gate does in this circuit
qc.h(0)Put qubit 0 in superposition |+⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2. It will measure 0 or 1 with equal probability.
qc.x(1)Flip qubit 1 from |0⟩ to |1⟩. It will always measure 1 unless another gate follows.
qc.cx(0, 2)CNOT with q0 as control, q2 as target. When q0 is |1⟩, flip q2. When q0 is |0⟩, leave q2 alone. This entangles q0 and q2.
qc.h(1)Apply H to qubit 1 (which is now in |1⟩). H|1⟩ = |−⟩ = (|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2. Still 50/50 probability but with a different phase.
qc.depth()Returns the number of gate layers. Our circuit has depth 3 H on q0 and X on q1 happen in parallel (depth 1), then CNOT (depth 2), then H on q1 and measurement (depth 3).

from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator from qiskit_aer.noise import NoiseModel, depolarizing_error import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # X·X = Identity → should always give |00⟩ qc = QuantumCircuit(2, 2) qc.x(0); qc.x(0) qc.x(1); qc.x(1) qc.measure([0,1], [0,1])

Run the circuit on the ideal simulator first no noise at all. The result should be |00⟩ with 100% probability, confirming the circuit is logically correct.

nb03_noise.pystep 2 ideal run
ideal_sim = AerSimulator() ideal_counts = ideal_sim.run( transpile(qc, ideal_sim), shots=1000 ).result().get_counts() print("Ideal (no noise):", ideal_counts)

Create a noise model that adds 1% depolarizing error to every single-qubit gate. Depolarizing noise randomly replaces a qubit's state with a completely random state, simulating the kind of imperfection found on real hardware.

nb03_noise.pystep 3 noise model
error_rate = 0.01 # try 0.001, 0.01, 0.05 to see the effect noise_model = NoiseModel() error = depolarizing_error(error_rate, 1) noise_model.add_all_qubit_quantum_error(error, ['x', 'h']) noisy_sim = AerSimulator(noise_model=noise_model)

nb04_transpile.pystep 2 transpile
backend = FakeNairobi() qc_transpiled = transpile(qc, backend, optimization_level=1) print("\n=== AFTER TRANSPILATION TO IBM NAIROBI ===") print(f"Depth: {qc_transpiled.depth()} Gates: {qc_transpiled.size()}") print(f"Native gates: {set(qc_transpiled.count_ops().keys())}") depth_increase = qc_transpiled.depth() / qc.depth() print(f"\nDepth increased by {depth_increase:.1f}× this is normal on real hardware.")
What transpilation does
FakeNairobi()A fake backend that simulates IBM's 7-qubit Nairobi chip its connectivity topology, native gate set, and noise properties. Lets you test transpilation without cloud access.
optimization_level=1Light optimisation. Level 0 = no optimisation (most depth inflation). Level 3 = heavy optimisation (slowest but shallowest result). For learning, use level 1.
qc_transpiled.depth()Depth after transpilation. Typically 2–4× the logical depth due to: native gate decomposition, SWAP insertion for connectivity, and basis changes.
📌 Expected result

Your logical circuit has depth ~4. After transpilation to Nairobi, expect depth ~8–16 depending on connectivity. The gate count typically increases from ~7 to ~20+. This is normal. Always check transpiled depth before running on real hardware if it exceeds ~50, the noise will dominate your results.
