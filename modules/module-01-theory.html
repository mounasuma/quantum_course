Module 01 · Foundation

Qubits & Superposition

The qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information. By the end of this module you will have written a qubit, put it in superposition, measured it 1000 times, and genuinely understood why the output is random.

⏱ ~2 hours 💻 Code included 🎯 Beginner

01 Start with something you already know the classical bit

Before we talk about qubits, let's make sure we truly understand the classical bit because the contrast is everything.

A classical bit is a physical object. In a modern computer, it is usually a tiny capacitor: charged means 1, discharged means 0. The bit has exactly one property that matters: its value. And that value is always either 0 or 1. There is no in-between. There is no uncertainty. There is no "both".

🧠 Analogy the light switch

A classical bit is a light switch. It is either off (0) or on (1). You can look at it right now and know exactly what it is. Flipping it takes energy and changes its state permanently. You can copy its state to another switch for free. You can read it as many times as you like without changing it.

These properties definite value, freely readable, freely copyable seem so obvious that we never think about them. But every single one of them breaks down at the quantum scale. And that is exactly what makes a qubit interesting.

02 What is a qubit, physically?

A qubit is any two-level quantum system a physical object that has exactly two distinguishable states, which we call |0⟩ and |1⟩. The angle brackets and the vertical bar are called Dirac notation we will use this throughout the course. For now, just read |0⟩ as "the zero state" and |1⟩ as "the one state".

Different quantum computers implement qubits in different physical ways:

Physical implementation What the qubit is Who uses it
Superconducting circuits Direction of current in a tiny superconducting loop IBM, Google, Rigetti
Trapped ions Energy levels of a single charged atom held by lasers IonQ, Quantinuum
Photonic Polarisation of a single photon Xanadu, PsiQuantum

The physical implementation does not change the mathematics or the code. When you write Qiskit code, you do not care whether the qubit is a current loop or a trapped ion the quantum operations work the same way. This is one of the beautiful things about quantum computing: the abstraction is clean.

💡 For this course

We will use superconducting qubits via IBM's Qiskit library. IBM provides free cloud access to real quantum hardware. But we will run most code on simulators first faster, noiseless, and free.

03 Superposition the idea, not the hype

Here is the property that makes a qubit different from a classical bit: a qubit can be in a superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩ simultaneously.

Before you've seen this before and thought "I don't really understand that" good. Most explanations skip the part that makes it actually click. Let's go slower.

A qubit's state is described by two numbers call them α and β such that:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩
The general state of a qubit. α and β are complex numbers.

α is the "amount of |0⟩" and β is the "amount of |1⟩". When you measure the qubit, you get 0 with probability |α|² and 1 with probability |β|². The only rule is that |α|² + |β|² = 1 (probabilities must sum to 1).

🧠 Analogy the spinning coin

A coin lying flat is a classical bit: definitely heads (0) or definitely tails (1). A coin spinning in the air is a qubit: it is in some combination of heads and tails simultaneously. The moment it lands the moment you measure it it becomes definitely heads or definitely tails. The probability of which outcome depends on the spin state.

This is not just an analogy. The mathematics is the same. The coin spinning is a genuine physical superposition. When you measure it (it lands), the superposition collapses to a definite outcome. This is called wavefunction collapse.

⚠ The most common misconception
✗  "Superposition means we don't know which state the qubit is in"
✓  Superposition means the qubit is genuinely in both states simultaneously it is not ignorance, it is physics. The qubit has no definite value until measured.

This distinction matters. "We don't know" is a statement about our knowledge. "Both simultaneously" is a statement about physical reality. Quantum mechanics verified by thousands of experiments says qubits are genuinely in superposition, not just unknown.

04 The Bloch sphere visualising a qubit

Any pure qubit state can be represented as a point on the surface of a sphere called the Bloch sphere. The north pole is |0⟩. The south pole is |1⟩. Every other point on the sphere is a superposition.

The Bloch sphere. Every qubit state is a point on its surface. The state vector |ψ⟩ points from the centre to that point.

The equator of the Bloch sphere represents states that are exactly half |0⟩ and half |1⟩ equal probability of each outcome when measured. The most important of these is the |+⟩ state, which is the state you get after applying a Hadamard gate to |0⟩. You will see this constantly.

For now, the main thing to understand from the Bloch sphere: a qubit's state is a direction, not a value. That direction encodes both the probability of each outcome and a phase relationship between the two states (which we will cover later in gates).

05 Your first quantum code create a qubit and measure it

Enough theory. Let's write quantum code. We will do three things:

1. Create a qubit in the |0⟩ state
2. Measure it immediately without doing anything
3. Observe what happens

python 01_classical_qubit.py measure |0⟩ directly
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator # Create a circuit: 1 qubit, 1 classical bit for the result qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) # Measure the qubit into the classical bit qc.measure(0, 0) # Run on a simulator, 1000 times sim = AerSimulator() job = sim.run(transpile(qc, sim), shots=1000) counts = job.result().get_counts() print(counts)
Output
{'0': 1000}
The qubit starts in |0⟩ by default. Every measurement gives 0. All 1000 times. No randomness.

This is the classical-like behaviour of a qubit. When it is in a definite state |0⟩ or |1⟩ it behaves like a classical bit. Measuring it gives the same answer every time.

Now let's create superposition.

python 02_superposition.py the Hadamard gate creates superposition
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) # The Hadamard gate H puts a qubit into equal superposition # |0⟩ becomes (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2 exactly 50% chance of each qc.h(0) qc.measure(0, 0) sim = AerSimulator() counts = sim.run(transpile(qc, sim), shots=1000).result().get_counts() print(counts)
Output run 1
{'0': 503, '1': 497}
Output run 2 (same code, run again)
{'0': 489, '1': 511}
Output run 3
{'0': 512, '1': 488}
Every run gives a different split but always close to 50/50. This is quantum randomness. Not because we don't know the answer but because the qubit genuinely had no definite value until measured.

This is the key result. The randomness is not a bug. It is not imprecision in the hardware. It is a fundamental property of quantum mechanics. The Hadamard gate put the qubit into an equal superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩. Measuring it collapsed the superposition to a random outcome with equal probability.

💡 Why this matters for quantum computing

If measuring always gives a random result, how is this useful? The answer is: by itself, it is not. The power comes from controlled superposition setting up a quantum state where the right answer has a high probability, and the wrong answers have low probability. That is what quantum algorithms do. You will see exactly how in Module 04 (Grover's algorithm).

06 Visualise the difference 1000 measurements

Let's visualise what is happening. We will measure both the classical bit and the qubit 1000 times and compare the distributions.

python 03_visualise.py classical vs quantum measurement
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator from qiskit.visualization import plot_histogram import matplotlib.pyplot as plt sim = AerSimulator() # Classical-like: no superposition qc_classical = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) qc_classical.measure(0, 0) counts_classical = sim.run(transpile(qc_classical, sim), shots=1000).result().get_counts() # Quantum: Hadamard superposition qc_quantum = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) qc_quantum.h(0) qc_quantum.measure(0, 0) counts_quantum = sim.run(transpile(qc_quantum, sim), shots=1000).result().get_counts() print("Classical-like |0⟩:", counts_classical) print("Quantum superposition:", counts_quantum) # Plot both histograms side by side fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 4)) plot_histogram(counts_classical, ax=ax1, title="Classical-like: |0⟩") plot_histogram(counts_quantum, ax=ax2, title="Quantum superposition: H|0⟩") plt.tight_layout() plt.savefig("qubit_comparison.png") plt.show()
Expected output
Classical-like |0⟩: {'0': 1000}
Quantum superposition: {'0': 502, '1': 498}
Left histogram: one bar at 0. Right histogram: two equal bars. This visual captures the entire difference between classical and quantum measurement.

07 Three properties that break your intuition

Before ending this module, there are three properties of qubits you must understand because they seem paradoxical but are absolutely true, and they will come up constantly.

1. Measurement destroys superposition permanently
Once you measure a qubit, its superposition collapses to a definite |0⟩ or |1⟩. The superposition is gone. You cannot measure it again to get the "other" value you will just get the same definite value you already have. This is why quantum algorithms must be carefully designed around when and how measurement happens.
2. You cannot copy a qubit the no-cloning theorem
In classical computing, copying a bit is trivial. In quantum computing, it is impossible. You cannot create a perfect copy of an unknown quantum state. This is a proven theorem not a hardware limitation. It is one reason quantum communication is inherently secure, and one reason quantum algorithms cannot simply "check and discard" like classical algorithms.
3. Quantum randomness is fundamentally different from classical randomness
When you flip a classical coin, the outcome is technically determined by physics air resistance, force, angle. In principle, if you knew all the variables, you could predict the outcome. Quantum measurement randomness is not like this. There is no hidden information that determines the outcome. The randomness is irreducible it is built into the structure of physical reality. Bell's theorem (1964) proved this experimentally.

A You will get 0 again, because you got 0 the first time
